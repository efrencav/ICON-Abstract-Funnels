The Positioning Framework

The five-part model we use to develop positioning for professional services firms — with prompts, examples, and the failure modes to avoid at each step.

By Abstract Creative For Founders & Principals Use with Leadership team Read time 20 min

What this framework is — and isn't

Positioning is not a tagline. It is not a one-liner you put on your homepage. It is the strategic decision that defines who you serve, what problem you solve for them, and why they should choose you over a comparable alternative. Every other brand decision — your messaging, your website, your marketing — flows from it.

This framework walks through each of the five components in sequence. Work through them in order. The prompts under each section are designed to pressure-test your thinking, not validate it. If a question is hard to answer, that difficulty is information.

1

Audience Specificity

Most positioning problems start here. Founders define their audience broadly because narrowing it feels like leaving revenue on the table. The opposite is true: the more specifically you can describe who you serve, the more resonant your positioning becomes — and the easier it is for the right clients to recognize themselves in it.

Audience specificity is not just demographics. It is the combination of who they are, where they are in their business, and what they are experiencing when they start looking for someone like you.

If your audience definition would apply to half the businesses in your city, it is not specific enough.

Define by asking

  • What type of firm or business?
  • What revenue stage or size?
  • What role are you selling to?
  • What situation are they in when they find you?
  • What have they already tried that didn't work?

Common failures

  • "Small to mid-size businesses"
  • "Companies in any industry"
  • Defining by geography alone
  • Defining by budget range, not situation
  • Changing the answer by who's in the room

Problem Definition

The problem you solve is not the service you provide. Clients don't hire you for the deliverable — they hire you because of what the deliverable does for them. The distinction matters because clients make decisions based on the problem they feel, not the solution you've designed.

Strong problem definitions describe the symptom the client is experiencing, not the category of service that addresses it. "Our brand isn't converting" is a problem. "You need brand strategy" is a category.

Define by asking

  • What symptom is the client experiencing?
  • What have they tried already?
  • What is the cost of not solving it?
  • What does it feel like from the inside?
  • What triggers them to start looking?

Common failures

  • Describing the service, not the problem
  • Using industry jargon the client doesn't use
  • Solving for the wrong audience's problem
  • Making the problem too broad to own
  • Confusing the symptom with the root cause

Write the core problem you solve — in your client's language

3

Differentiator Structure

Your differentiator answers one question: why you, specifically, over a comparable alternative? Not why you're better — why you're different in a way that matters to the audience you just defined.

Most differentiator claims fail one of two tests. The first is the competitor test: could your top competitor say the same thing? If yes, it's a category claim, not a differentiator. The second is the relevance test: does the difference actually matter to the client you described in Component 1? If not, it's a feature, not a position.

A differentiator is not what makes you better. It is what makes you different in a way that matters to the specific client you're trying to attract.

Structure your differentiator

  • What do you do that competitors don't?
  • What do you refuse to do that others do?
  • What unique method, model, or approach do you use?
  • What do you know that others don't?
  • Who do you serve that others overlook?

Differentiators that aren't

  • "We really care about our clients"
  • "We deliver results"
  • "We have over X years of experience"
  • "We're a full-service firm"
  • "We treat you like a partner, not a client"

Write your differentiator — then run it through both tests

Competitor test: Could a competitor say this?

Relevance test: Does it matter to your audience?

Proof

Positioning without proof is a claim. Proof is what makes a positioning believable — and believability is what determines whether a prospective client acts on it or dismisses it.

Proof comes in several forms, and each serves a different function. The most credible proof is outcome-based: specific results, named clients, and transformations that a prospective client can map to their own situation. Social proof (testimonials, logos) adds credibility. Methodological proof (a proprietary process, a named framework) signals rigor.

Types of proof to inventory

  • Named case studies with outcomes
  • Specific results (time, revenue, growth)
  • Client testimonials with context
  • Industry recognition or credentials
  • Proprietary methods or frameworks
  • Years of focused experience in the niche

Weak proof patterns

  • Generic testimonials with no specifics
  • Results without client context
  • "Trusted by hundreds of clients"
  • Awards unrecognized by target clients
  • Proof that doesn't match the audience

Message Translation

The first four components are strategic. This one is operational. Message translation is the process of converting your positioning into the actual language your team uses — across your website, proposals, sales conversations, and content.

The goal is a positioning statement and a set of core messages that can be used by anyone on your team, consistently, without interpretation. If your team has to guess how to say what you do — you haven't finished this step.

What to translate it into

  • One-sentence firm description
  • Elevator pitch (30 sec, spoken)
  • Homepage headline and subheadline
  • Proposal opening paragraph
  • Email introduction for new prospects
  • Team member "what do you do" answer

Translation failures

  • Jargon the client doesn't use
  • Different language on every channel
  • Messaging that describes the firm, not the client's problem
  • Positioning that lives only in the founder's head

Draft your one-sentence firm description using the components above

We help [specific audience] who are dealing with [defined problem]
by [your differentiator] — so they can [outcome].

This formula is a starting point, not a final sentence. The language should sound like how you actually talk — not a fill-in-the-blank template. Use it to pressure-test structure, then rewrite it in your voice.

Example — How the five components come together

Audience
Founders of professional services firms at $500K–$5M who are growing past the point where their personal reputation closes every deal — and whose brand hasn't caught up to the business they're now running.
Problem
Their brand was built for a version of the business that no longer exists. It depends on the founder to work. When they're not in the room, the messaging drifts, the team improvises, and marketing spend stops converting.
Differentiator
We don't just design a brand — we install the strategic infrastructure behind it: positioning, messaging architecture, and governance systems that let the brand operate independently of the founder.
Proof
16+ case studies across professional services, B2B, and industrial sectors. Clients include firms that have scaled from founder-led to team-led brand execution within 18 months of engagement.
Translation
"We help professional services firms at the $500K–$5M stage build the brand infrastructure they need to scale — so the business stops depending on the founder to communicate, sell, and deliver on brand."
